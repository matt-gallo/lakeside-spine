Needles Aren't Just Needles

"Dr. Andrew, isn't this just acupuncture?" Both involve needles, but the similarities end there.

Functional dry needling goes deep – longer needles penetrating into muscle tissue itself. I'm targeting trigger points and working directly with your muscular and nervous systems based on Western medicine's understanding of anatomy.

Acupuncture works with meridian systems and energy flow using more superficial needles. It's a completely different philosophy requiring years of traditional medicine study.

The Choice

If you're afraid of needles, neither works for you. But if you're open to it: Do you want someone working with your muscles and nerves directly (dry needling), or energy meridians (acupuncture)? I'm wired more like a mechanic, so dry needling fits my practice better.

Tech Neck and the Modern Spine Disaster

I'm sitting up straighter right now just thinking about this. When I started practicing, it was construction workers with back pain. Now? It's upper back and neck issues from computers and phones. We've created new problems with modern life.

And yes, dry needling is incredibly effective for this.

Your postural muscles are built for endurance – they should keep you upright all day. But when you slump at your desk or crane your neck at your phone, those muscles turn off. Meanwhile, your pecs get tight, shoulders round forward.

Dry needling can wake up those sleeping postural muscles and calm down the overactive ones pulling you into bad posture. It's like having a conversation with your nervous system: "Hey, remember how you're supposed to hold your shoulders back?"
